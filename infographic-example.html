Section 01 · Overview

Industrial policy was dead
for forty years. It isn't anymore.

After four decades in which Western governments treated active industrial policy as an economic heresy, a quiet reversal has taken shape. Across the United States, the European Union, Japan, and South Korea, trillion-dollar subsidy packages, protected supply chains, and state-directed investment have returned — not as rhetoric but as law. This briefing traces how it happened, what is driving it, and what a post-neoliberal industrial consensus looks like.

Section 02 · Timeline

Four decades in six moments

The arc from the Washington Consensus to the current moment was not linear. Each inflection was a response to a specific shock — the financial crisis, the supply-chain disruptions of 2020, the strategic competition with China — and each layered new assumptions onto the policy stack without ever publicly repudiating the old ones.

1989 — Washington Consensus

A codified anti-industrial-policy orthodoxy

John Williamson's ten-point list becomes the IMF's de facto lending conditionality. Industrial policy is named explicitly as a distortion to be unwound.

2008 — Global financial crisis

The first quiet return

Auto bailouts, bank rescues, and green-stimulus packages cross the line from "rescue" to "directed investment" — though the framing remains exceptional, temporary, and apologetic.

2015 — Made in China 2025

The external forcing function

Beijing publishes a decade-long industrial strategy targeting ten sectors. Western policymakers begin drafting responses framed as "competitiveness", not industrial policy.

2020 — Supply-chain shock

The legitimacy crisis of just-in-time

Pandemic disruptions expose the fragility of globally distributed production. "Resilience" replaces "efficiency" as the organizing principle of supply-chain thinking.

2022 — CHIPS Act & IRA

America crosses the Rubicon

Two bills totaling $670B in subsidies, tax credits, and directed procurement make the return of U.S. industrial policy explicit and legislated — the first time since 1945.

2024-2026 — Global diffusion

The policy becomes the consensus

The EU's Net Zero Industry Act, Japan's economic security law, and Korea's K-CHIPS Act follow in rapid succession. What began as an American response becomes the new multilateral norm.

The question this raises. If industrial policy is back, what does its return actually look like in numbers — and are the economic concerns that killed it in the 1980s still valid today?

Section 03 · Evidence

The scale, measured

The return of industrial policy is not rhetorical. Tracked commitments across OECD economies have grown by more than an order of magnitude since 2015, driven almost entirely by three sectors: semiconductors, clean energy, and critical minerals.

What is striking is the concentration. The top five programs — U.S. IRA, CHIPS Act, EU Green Deal Industrial Plan, Japan's economic security package, and Korea's K-CHIPS — account for more than three-quarters of the global total. The remainder is diffuse.

The policy instruments have also narrowed. Tax credits and production subsidies dominate; traditional state ownership is rare. This is industrial policy delivered through the tax code, not through state-owned enterprises.

Section 04 · Comparison

Five programs, one logic

Despite differences in legal architecture and political vocabulary, the five major programs converge on a remarkably similar design: a preferred sector list, tax-credit-based delivery, local-content conditionalities, and a decade-long horizon.

Section 05 · Scenarios

Three ways this ends

The current moment is unstable. A programmatic return of industrial policy at this scale will either become the new normal, be partially rolled back as specific programs fail visibly, or accelerate into something closer to open economic nationalism. Each path has a different set of antecedents and different implications for the WTO framework.

Scenario 01

Base case · ~55% probability

Codified pluralism

Industrial policy becomes a permanent fixture of economic governance, coexisting uneasily with residual WTO discipline. The dispute-resolution system adapts, carve-outs proliferate, and the architecture holds.

Scenario 02

Downside · ~25% probability

Programmatic retreat

One or more flagship programs fails visibly — a politically salient factory closes, a subsidy is captured by incumbents — and the political economy of industrial policy collapses faster than it was built.

Scenario 03

Upside (for nationalists) · ~20% probability

Bloc formation

Subsidy competition triggers formal economic blocs. Trade discipline fragments along geopolitical lines. The WTO becomes ornamental. This is the path where "industrial policy" becomes "economic statecraft."

Strategic implication

The analytical question for the next decade is not whether industrial policy has returned, but whether it can be disciplined enough to avoid the capture and waste that killed its predecessor — without being disciplined so heavily that it ceases to function at all.
